Investment Thesis Creep

Checking Your Heavy Positions for "Thesis Creep"

Structure

"Thesis Creep" is a critical psychological trap in investing. It describes the behavior where an investor constantly modifies or replaces their original Investment Thesis just to justify holding onto an underperforming stock.

01

What is an Investment Thesis?

An investment thesis is the explicit rationale—often written down—for buying a stock. It answers the core question: "Why do I believe this company or stock will deliver excess returns in the future?"

Example

"I am buying Company A because it is the fastest-growing SaaS company in emerging markets, with Annual Recurring Revenue (ARR) projected to grow at 30% per year."

02

What is Thesis Creep?

When a company or stock underperforms, or when the initial investment hypothesis is proven wrong, the disciplined move is to sell. However, due to Cognitive Biases, investors often refuse to admit the mistake and realize the loss.

Instead, they fabricate a new reason to support holding the position.

Think of the initial investment rationale as an anchor. As time passes and bad news accumulates, instead of holding firm, that anchor is dragged—"creeping"—to a completely different location.

03

How Professionals Avoid This Trap

Write It Down

Before buying, explicitly document the core reasons for the investment in writing, along with the specific metrics for verification.

Define Killers

Clearly list within the thesis which specific events or data points would prove the original thesis invalid. If these occur, it triggers a mandatory sell.

Periodic Review

Review the original investment thesis annually or semi-annually. If the company's current reality is far removed from the original thesis, and you find yourself needing a new, completely different reason to justify holding it, that is usually a sell signal.

04

Summary

In summary: Thesis Creep is the act of making excuses for failed investments.

It is a hallmark of a lack of investment discipline and a failure to face market changes head-on. Maintaining intellectual honesty about why you own an asset is the primary defense against significant portfolio drawdowns.
